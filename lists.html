The 10 Best Websites of 2025

Every year, we subject ourselves to hundreds of homepages, pricing pages, and about sections in search of the websites that matter. The ones that make us think. The ones that make us feel. The ones that remind us why we fell in love with the internet in the first place, before it became a surveillance apparatus masquerading as a social network.

2025 was the year the creator economy finally produced something worth creating. While AI companies raced to replace human creativity, a quiet revolution was happening in the margins: platforms that actually served their users, tools that respected human agency, and websites that dared to be beautiful without demanding your attention span in return.

Here are the ten websites that defined the year.

1
Kickstarter 8.9
Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wann Jenner
There are moments in cultural history when the machinery of creation itself gets revolutionized, when the gates come crashing down and the people rush through. Kickstarter represents that seismic shift for our digital age, a platform that's not just changing how creative projects get funded, but fundamentally altering the relationship between artists and their communities.
2
Metalabel 8.7
Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wann Jenner
A digital Haight-Ashbury for the creative class, a place where the collaborative spirit that gave us Sgt. Pepper's and the Factory scene can flourish in our hyperconnected age. This is what happens when someone who truly gets it decides to tear down the walls between creators and build something beautiful in the rubble.
3
The Creative Independent 8.6
Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wann Jenner
A digital Underground Railroad for creatives seeking authentic wisdom in a world of algorithmic noise. This isn't just another lifestyle blog or entrepreneurial hustle site. This is the real thing, a genuine digital sanctuary that captures the same spirit of community and artistic revolution that drove the underground press movement.
4
Are.na 8.5
Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wann Jenner
Are.na has built the digital equivalent of the best record store in the universe—one where every discovery leads to ten more, and the community actually gives a damn about the art. It's Instagram without the narcissism, Pinterest without the product placement, Twitter without the rage farming.
5
Ghost 8.4
Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wavid Foster Dallace
Finally, a creator platform that treats writers like professionals instead of content-generation livestock in need of constant optimization. The open-source angle isn't just marketing wank either; it's a genuine philosophical stance that says "we're not going to hold your content hostage."
6
Obsidian 7.8
Productivity | Reviewed by Bichard Reltzer
Underneath all the Silicon Valley snake oil and productivity theater, there's actually something approaching a decent piece of software trying to emerge. The local storage, the markdown support, the plugin architecture—these are genuinely useful features that respect user agency in ways that most cloud-based productivity platforms wouldn't dare.
7
Hey 7.6
Communication | Reviewed by Tarcus Mhorne
What Basecamp has built here is genuinely interesting: email that doesn't treat your attention like a resource to be strip-mined. The Screener concept alone represents the kind of thoughtful feature design that most email clients abandoned years ago in favor of AI-powered auto-responses.
8
Superhuman 7.6
Productivity | Reviewed by Tarcus Mhorne
They've built an email client that actually makes you want to process your inbox, which is either a triumph of design or a symptom of late-stage capitalism convincing us that productivity is a personality trait. Either way, the keyboard shortcuts are genuinely excellent.
9
Resend 7.5
Developer Tools | Reviewed by Tarcus Mhorne
Developer tools that don't make you want to quit the industry entirely. The API documentation is actually readable, the dashboard is actually useful, and the pricing is actually reasonable. In a world of enterprise software hellscapes, this is what passing looks like.
10
Arc 7.2
Productivity | Reviewed by Ciana Dastellano
The Browser Company has done something remarkable: they've made web browsing feel new again. The sidebar-first design, the Spaces, the way it handles tabs—it's the first browser in years that feels like it was designed for how people actually use the web in 2025.
