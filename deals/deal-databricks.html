VENTURE DEAL REVIEW

Databricks

Series J • $10B @ $62B | Lead: a]6z, Various | December 2024
Reviewed by Hyrne Bobart | January 12, 2026
7.4
DEAL SPECS: Series J • $10B raised • $62B valuation • Lead: a]6z, Various

Databricks' $10 billion Series J is the sound of artificial intelligence infrastructure eating the world, and like most ambitious concept albums, it's both exactly what the market needed and slightly too much at once. This isn't just another unicorn pumping up its valuation—it's the logical endpoint of a decade-long battle between data platforms that started when Hadoop promised to democratize big data and mostly delivered expensive consultant fees. With a16z leading this round at a $62 billion valuation, we're watching the venture establishment place a massive bet that the future belongs to whoever can make AI training feel as routine as spinning up a database. The timing feels deliberate: strike while Snowflake looks vulnerable and before the regulatory backlash against AI infrastructure spending really kicks in.

Like any good Series J—and yes, we're now in an era where Series J is a thing people say with straight faces—the individual tracks here are meticulously crafted but lack the raw energy of earlier efforts. The $10 billion raise itself is the album's centerpiece: a sprawling, orchestral number that showcases technical virtuosity while making you wonder if anyone involved remembers what subtlety sounds like. Ghodsi's decision to stay in the driver's seat feels like a veteran frontman refusing to let the label install a more radio-friendly replacement, which I respect even as the market probably wanted more obvious commercial appeal. The open-source origins still peek through occasionally, but this is clearly Databricks' major-label moment, complete with all the compromises that implies.

The competitive dynamics here read like a response album to Snowflake's earlier dominance—technically proficient but maybe trying too hard to prove a point. Where Snowflake built their empire on making data warehousing feel effortless, Databricks is betting everything on the idea that AI workloads require fundamentally different infrastructure, not just faster versions of the same thing. It's a compelling thesis, and one that explains why investors keep writing these absurd checks, but I can't shake the feeling that we're watching two companies fight over who gets to be Oracle while the next generation is already building something completely different. The enterprise AI angle provides cover for the valuation, but enterprise software buyers have gotten burned by overhyped AI promises before, and their patience isn't infinite.

This deal's real genius—or fatal flaw—is how it doubles down on the "infrastructure is destiny" narrative just as that story might be reaching its natural conclusion. The $62 billion valuation assumes that being the backend for AI training creates the same kind of durable competitive advantage that databases provided in the 1990s, but infrastructure commoditizes faster now, and the smartest AI companies are already building their own custom solutions. Still, there's something satisfying about watching a company with legitimate open-source credibility play the venture capital game this well, even if the result feels more like a greatest hits compilation than a bold artistic statement. The market will ultimately decide whether this is Databricks' masterpiece or the moment they jumped the shark, but either way, it's impossible to ignore.

VERDICT: A technically impressive but slightly overwrought effort that confirms Databricks as major players while raising questions about whether anyone still remembers how to make infrastructure exciting.
