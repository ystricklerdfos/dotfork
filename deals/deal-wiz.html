VENTURE DEAL REVIEW

Wiz

Rejected Acquisition • $23B @ $23B (rejected) | Lead: Google | July 2024
Reviewed by Tarcus Mhorne | January 12, 2026
8.1
Best New Deal
DEAL SPECS: Rejected Acquisition • $23B raised • $23B (rejected) valuation • Lead: Google

Wiz just dropped the most audacious power move in the cloud security scene since CrowdStrike's 2019 IPO run, and honestly, I'm here for it. Turning down Google's $23 billion acquisition offer—the largest enterprise software deal ever proposed—takes the kind of founder hubris that either births unicorn legends or spectacular flame-outs. The Israeli quartet behind this outfit has orchestrated something genuinely unprecedented: rejecting guaranteed liquidity at a 23x revenue multiple to chase public market glory. It's giving me serious "difficult third album" energy, where the band gets too big for their original label and decides to go independent. The timing feels both perfectly calculated and dangerously ambitious, especially with the IPO market still nursing a brutal hangover from 2022's correction.

The fundamentals backing this rejection are admittedly stellar—this is no vanity play masquerading as strategy. Wiz's sprint to $100M ARR in just four years makes Snowflake's early trajectory look pedestrian, and their 120% net revenue retention suggests genuine product-market fit rather than venture-subsidized growth. The cloud security market timing couldn't be more favorable, with enterprise CISO budgets expanding faster than a crypto bull run. But here's where I start questioning the mix: Google's $23B offer wasn't just generous, it was historically unprecedented for a company this young. The decision to walk away assumes they can command an even richer public valuation, which requires threading an impossibly narrow needle between market conditions, execution, and investor sentiment.

What fascinates me about this "deal" is how the rejection itself becomes the story—a meta-commentary on founder confidence in an era where most startups would kill for half this exit multiple. The optics are undeniably powerful: Israeli entrepreneurs schooling Mountain View's finest on company valuation, essentially telling Google their money isn't good enough. It's the venture equivalent of an artist turning down a major label contract to prove they can headline Coachella independently. The risk-reward calculus here is genuinely thrilling, even if it makes my inner spreadsheet-obsessed analyst break out in cold sweats. They're betting on public markets rewarding their growth trajectory with an even richer multiple than Google's already generous offer.

Still, I can't shake the feeling that this move carries serious downside protection issues that the hype cycle is conveniently ignoring. Public market valuations for high-growth SaaS companies have compressed dramatically—even market darlings like Zoom and DocuSign are trading at fractions of their pandemic peaks. Wiz is essentially wagering that their IPO timing will coincide with renewed public market appetite for richly valued cloud security plays, which feels optimistic given current macro headwinds. The execution risk is non-trivial: they need to maintain hypergrowth metrics while navigating SOX compliance, public company governance, and quarterly earnings pressure. One stumble in growth trajectory or a broader market correction could make Google's rejected offer look prophetic rather than inadequate.

VERDICT: A bold sophomore statement that either cements legendary status or becomes a cautionary tale about leaving money on the table.
