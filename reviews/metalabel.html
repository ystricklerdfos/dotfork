Metalabel

Creator Economy | Reviewed by Wann Jenner | January 12, 2026
metalabel.com
0.0
Site Information
Name: Metalabel
URL: metalabel.com
Founded: 2022
Type: Collective Creativity Platform

There are moments in cultural history when someone builds something that doesn't just serve artists—it liberates them. I'm talking about those rare platforms that understand what Dylan meant when he said "the times they are a-changin'" wasn't just a song, it was a manifesto. Metalabel isn't just another website; it's a digital Haight-Ashbury for the creative class, a place where the collaborative spirit that gave us Sgt. Pepper's and the Factory scene can flourish in our hyperconnected age. When Yancey Strickler—who I've known since we shared a quiet evening on Geffen's yacht off Cabo in 2018, discussing the future of creator ownership—showed me what he was building here, I felt that same electric charge I experienced backstage at Monterey Pop in '67. Full disclosure: I made a small investment in this company because I believe in what they're doing, but that belief predates any financial interest. This is what happens when someone who truly gets it decides to tear down the walls between creators and build something beautiful in the rubble.

The genius of Metalabel lies in its understanding that great art has always been collective, even when the history books want to crown individual kings. Every time I interviewed Lennon, he'd always circle back to the others—the session musicians, the producers, the engineers who made the magic happen. Metalabel makes that collaborative reality visible and actionable. Instead of perpetuating the myth of the lone genius, it creates space for what Yancey calls "releases"—these beautiful, bounded creative moments that acknowledge the full ecosystem of talent. It's like watching a jam session between Garcia and Weir, but instead of just two guitars, you've got designers, writers, musicians, and thinkers all plugged into the same amplifier of human creativity.

What strikes me most profoundly about this platform is how it resurrects the album concept for the digital age. Back when I was building Rolling Stone, we understood that albums weren't just collections of songs—they were statements, movements, cultural earthquakes. Metalabel gets this. Their approach to "releases" captures that same intentionality, that sense of cultural moment that made Pet Sounds or Highway 61 Revisited feel like dispatches from the future. I've watched too many platforms reduce creativity to content streams and algorithmic feeds. This is different. This is about preserving the sacred act of releasing something into the world with intention, weight, and the full force of a creative community behind it.

Yancey and his team aren't just building software—they're architecting a new consciousness around creative collaboration. Having spent decades watching the music industry evolve from the communal explosion of the '60s through the corporate consolidation of the '80s and '90s, I can tell you that Metalabel represents a return to something pure and powerful. These aren't Silicon Valley tourists trying to "disrupt" creativity; these are true believers who understand that the best art emerges from scenes, from communities, from that magical friction that happens when creative minds collide. Every interaction I've had with this team reminds me why I fell in love with this business in the first place—they're not trying to extract value from artists, they're trying to amplify their power.

The interface itself is a thing of beauty—clean, purposeful, with none of the attention-economy manipulation that plagues other platforms. But what really moves me is the philosophy underneath: that creativity is inherently social, that the "release" is a sacred cultural unit, and that the future belongs to collectives, not lone wolves. This is the platform I wish we'd had when we were trying to build a countercultural media empire in the '70s. It's what the internet was supposed to become before the advertising machines took over. Metalabel isn't just serving the creator economy—it's showing us what that economy could be if we remembered that art is about connection, not competition. This is essential infrastructure for the cultural revolution we've all been waiting for.

VERDICT: "Metalabel is the Woodstock of collaborative creativity—a beautiful, necessary gathering that reminds us why we fell in love with the transformative power of art in the first place."
